Options Tutorial: A Beginner's Guide

Andrew Zhu February 17, 2025

Options are contracts that give you the right, but not the obligation, to buy or sell an asset (like a stock) at a specific price (the strike price) on or before a specific date (the expiration date). Think of it like a reservation – you’re reserving the right to buy something at a certain price, but you don’t have to buy it.

Key Terms:

  • Underlying Asset: The asset the option contract is based on (e.g., a stock like AAPL).
  • Call Option: Gives the buyer the right to buy the underlying asset.
  • Put Option: Gives the buyer the right to sell the underlying asset.
  • Strike Price: The price at which you can buy (for a call) or sell (for a put) the underlying asset if you exercise the option.
  • Expiration Date: The date the option contract expires. After this date, the option is no longer valid.
  • Premium: The price you pay to buy an option contract. This is what the seller receives.
  • Option Buyer (Holder): The person who buys the option.
  • Option Seller (Writer): The person who sells the option. They receive the premium.
  • Exercise: The act of using your right to buy (call) or sell (put) the underlying asset at the strike price.
  • In the Money (ITM): A call option is ITM when the current market price of the underlying asset is above the strike price. A put option is ITM when the current market price is below the strike price.
  • At the Money (ATM): When the market price is equal to the strike price.
  • Out of the Money (OTM): A call option is OTM when the market price is below the strike price. A put option is OTM when the market price is above the strike price.

Types of Options:

  • American Options: Can be exercised anytime before the expiration date.
  • European Options: Can only be exercised on the expiration date. Most exchange-traded options are American.

Why Use Options?

  • Leverage: Options allow you to control a large number of shares with a smaller investment (the premium).
  • Hedging: Options can be used to protect your existing stock portfolio from potential losses.
  • Income Generation: Selling options can generate income (the premium received).
  • Speculation: Options can be used to bet on the direction of a stock’s price movement.

Example:

Let’s say AAPL is trading at $150. You think the price will go up.

  • Buying a Call Option: You buy a AAPL call option with a strike price of $155 and an expiration date in one month. Let’s say the premium is $5 per share (options contracts represent 100 shares). Your total cost would be $500 (100 shares x $5 premium).

    • If AAPL goes above $155 before expiration, your option is ITM and you can exercise it (buy AAPL at $155) and potentially profit.
    • If AAPL stays below $155, your option expires worthless, and you lose your $500 premium.

  • Buying a Put Option: You buy a AAPL put option with a strike price of $145 and the same expiration date. Let’s say the premium is $3. Your cost is $300.

    • If AAPL goes below $145, your put option is ITM and you can exercise it (sell AAPL at $145) and potentially profit.
    • If AAPL stays above $145, your option expires worthless, and you lose your $300 premium.

Important Considerations:

  • Risk: Options trading is risky. You can lose your entire investment (the premium).
  • Time Decay: Options lose value over time, especially as the expiration date approaches. This is called time decay or theta.
  • Complexity: Options strategies can be complex and require a good understanding of market dynamics.

Next Steps:

  • Paper Trading: Practice trading options with a virtual trading account to get a feel for how they work without risking real money.
  • Research: Learn as much as you can about different options strategies.
  • Start Small: If you decide to trade options, start with a small amount of capital that you can afford to lose.

This tutorial provides a basic overview. Options trading is a complex subject, and further research is essential. Don’t hesitate to ask if you have more questions!
