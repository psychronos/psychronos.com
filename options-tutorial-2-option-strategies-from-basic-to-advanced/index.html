Options Tutorial #2: Option Strategies - From Basic to Advanced

Andrew Zhu February 17, 2025

Now that we’ve covered the fundamentals of options, it’s time to explore the diverse strategies available to options traders. This section will guide you through various approaches, from simple buying and selling to more complex combinations, illustrating their applications with real-world examples using popular stocks like AAPL and TSLA.

1. Basic Strategies: Laying the Groundwork

These strategies are the building blocks of options trading. Understanding them thoroughly is essential before moving on to more complex techniques.

1.1. Buying Calls

This is the most straightforward bullish strategy. You buy a call option when you expect the underlying asset’s price to rise. Your profit potential is theoretically unlimited, while your maximum loss is limited to the premium you paid.

  • Example (AAPL): Suppose AAPL is trading at $150. You believe it will rise. You buy an AAPL call option with a strike price of $155 and a premium of $5. If AAPL rises to $165 before expiration, your call option is now worth at least $10 (intrinsic value). You can sell the option for a profit or exercise it to buy AAPL at $155. If AAPL stays below $155, your option will expire worthless, and you lose the $5 premium.

1.2. Buying Puts

This is the basic bearish strategy. You buy a put option when you expect the underlying asset’s price to fall. Your profit potential is substantial, limited only by the underlying price falling to zero, and your maximum loss is the premium paid.

  • Example (TSLA): TSLA is trading at $200. You anticipate a price drop. You buy a TSLA put option with a strike price of $190 and a premium of $7. If TSLA falls to $180, your put option gains at least $10 in intrinsic value. You can sell the option for a profit or exercise it to sell TSLA at $190. If TSLA remains above $190, your put will expire worthless, limiting your loss to the $7 premium.

1.3. Covered Calls

This strategy involves owning shares of a stock and selling call options against those shares. It’s used to generate income (the premium received) and potentially sell your shares at a higher price. It’s best used in neutral to slightly bullish markets.

  • Example (AAPL): You own 100 shares of AAPL at $150. You sell one AAPL call option contract (covering 100 shares) with a strike price of $160 and receive a premium of $3 per share ($300 total). If AAPL stays below $160, you keep the premium and your shares. If AAPL rises above $160, your shares may be called away, but you’ll have sold them at a higher price plus the premium received.

1.4. Protective Puts

This strategy involves owning shares of stock and buying put options for the same stock. It’s used to protect your stock holdings from potential price declines. It acts like an insurance policy.

  • Example (TSLA): You own 100 shares of TSLA at $200. You buy one TSLA put option contract (covering 100 shares) with a strike price of $190 and pay a premium of $8 per share ($800 total). If TSLA stays above $190, your put expires, and you lose the premium, but your stock is still worth more. If TSLA falls below $190, your put option gains value, offsetting some or all of the losses in your stock position.

2. Intermediate Strategies: Combining Options

These strategies involve using two or more options simultaneously, allowing for more nuanced market predictions and risk management. Let’s delve deeper into these important options strategies:

2.1. Spreads: Profiting from Relative Price Movement

Spreads involve simultaneously buying and selling options of the same type (calls or puts) on the same underlying asset but with different characteristics. The goal is to profit from the relative price movement of the options, rather than just the directional movement of the underlying asset. Spreads are often used to reduce the cost of a directional trade or to profit from specific market conditions.

  • Vertical Spreads: These spreads use options with the same expiration date but different strike prices.

    • Bull Call Spread: You buy a call option with a lower strike price and sell a call option with a higher strike price. This strategy is bullish but limits your profit potential. It’s cheaper than simply buying a call option.

      • Example (NVDA): NVDA is trading at $300. You buy a NVDA call option with a $305 strike price and sell a NVDA call option with a $310 strike price. Your maximum profit is the difference in strike prices ($5) minus the net debit you paid for the spread. Your maximum loss is the net debit you paid. This strategy profits if NVDA rises but caps your profit if it goes significantly above $310.

    • Bear Put Spread: You buy a put option with a higher strike price and sell a put option with a lower strike price. This strategy is bearish but limits your profit potential. It’s cheaper than simply buying a put option.

      • Example (AMD): AMD is trading at $100. You buy an AMD put option with a $105 strike price and sell a AMD put option with a $100 strike price. Your maximum profit is the difference in strike prices ($5) minus the net debit you paid for the spread. Your maximum loss is the net debit paid. This strategy profits if AMD falls but limits your profit if it goes significantly below $100.

    • Other Vertical Spreads: There are variations like ratio spreads (different numbers of options bought and sold) and diagonal spreads (different strikes and expirations).

  • Horizontal Spreads (Calendar Spreads): These spreads use options with the same strike price but different expiration dates. They are used to profit from the difference in time decay (theta) between near-term and longer-term options or from changes in implied volatility.

    • Example (BABA): BABA is trading at $90. You sell a BABA call option expiring in one month with a $90 strike price and buy a BABA call option expiring in two months with the same $90 strike price. You profit if the implied volatility increases, or if the near-term option decays faster than the longer-term option. This strategy is often used in a range-bound market.

  • Diagonal Spreads: These are a combination of vertical and horizontal spreads – they involve different strike prices and different expiration dates. They are more complex and offer greater flexibility in tailoring the strategy to your specific market outlook.

2.2. Straddles and Strangles: Betting on Volatility

Straddles and strangles are volatility plays. You use them when you expect a significant price move in the underlying asset but are unsure of the direction.

  • Straddle: You buy both a call and a put option on the same underlying asset with the same strike price and expiration date.

    • Example (NFLX): NFLX is trading at $350. You buy a NFLX call option with a $350 strike price and a NFLX put option with the same $350 strike price and the same expiration date. Your profit potential is unlimited in either direction, but you need a large move to cover the cost of both premiums. You lose the entire premium you paid if no significant price movement occurs.

  • Strangle: You buy both a call and a put option on the same underlying asset with different strike prices but the same expiration date. The call’s strike price is above the current market price, and the put’s strike price is below the current market price.

    • Example (GOOG): GOOG is trading at $2500. You buy a GOOG call option with a $2550 strike price and a GOOG put option with a $2450 strike price. Your profit potential is unlimited in either direction, but you need a large move to cover the cost of the premiums and the difference in strikes. Strangles are generally cheaper than straddles but require a larger price move to become profitable.

2.3. Iron Condors and Butterflies: Targeting a Range

These strategies are used when you expect the underlying asset’s price to remain within a specific range.

  • Iron Condor: You sell an out-of-the-money (OTM) call and an OTM put. To cap your potential losses, you also buy an OTM call with a higher strike price and an OTM put with a lower strike price. This strategy has a limited profit potential (the net credit received when opening the position) but also a limited risk.

    • Example (MSFT): MSFT is trading at $250. You sell a MSFT $260 call and a MSFT $240 put. You also buy a MSFT $265 call and a MSFT $235 put. You profit if MSFT stays between $240 and $260.

  • Butterfly: You buy one call at a low strike, sell two calls at a middle strike, and buy one call at a high strike. Or the same operation with puts. This strategy has limited profit potential and limited risk, profiting most when the underlying price is near the middle strike price.

    • Example (AMZN): AMZN is trading at $150. You buy one AMZN $145 call, sell two AMZN $150 calls, and buy one AMZN $155 call. You profit most if AMZN stays close to $150.

2.4. Important Considerations for All Strategies:

  • Risk Management: Options trading involves risk. Always understand your potential profit and loss before entering a trade.
  • Commissions: Consider the impact of commissions on your overall profitability.
  • Time Decay: Time decay (theta) works against option buyers and in favor of option sellers.
  • Implied Volatility: Changes in implied volatility can significantly impact option prices.
  • Liquidity: Ensure the options you are trading have sufficient liquidity (open interest and volume).

Remember, these are just a few of the many option strategies available. The world of options trading is vast and complex. Continued learning, practice, and experience are crucial for success. Start with paper trading or small positions to gain experience before risking significant capital.

3. Advanced Strategies: Refining Your Approach

These strategies represent a significant step up in complexity from basic option buying and selling, demanding a more profound grasp of the intricate mechanics governing option pricing and behavior. Beyond simply understanding the directional implications of calls and puts, traders employing these advanced techniques must possess a nuanced appreciation for how factors like time decay (theta), implied volatility (vega), and the underlying asset’s price sensitivity (delta) interact and influence the value of multi-leg positions. Furthermore, effective utilization of these strategies hinges on a robust and disciplined approach to risk management, as the potential for both profit and loss can be magnified compared to simpler trades. This includes meticulously calculating maximum profit and loss potential, carefully considering the probabilities of various market scenarios, and establishing clear exit strategies to mitigate potential downside risks. Without this comprehensive understanding and rigorous risk assessment, venturing into these more sophisticated options strategies can expose traders to substantial financial peril.

3.1. Calendar Spreads and Ratio Spreads

  • Calendar Spreads (Horizontal Spreads): A calendar spread involves simultaneously buying and selling options contracts of the same type (either calls or puts), with the same strike price, but with different expiration dates. The goal is to profit from the difference in how the options’ prices change over time (time decay) or from changes in implied volatility.

    • How it works: Typically, you sell a near-term option and buy a longer-term option. The near-term option will decay in value faster than the longer-term option. If the underlying price stays relatively stable, the short option will lose value more quickly, and you profit from this difference. You can also profit if implied volatility increases, as longer-term options are generally more sensitive to volatility changes.

    • Types:

      • Diagonal Calendar Spread: While technically a diagonal spread, it shares similar properties to a calendar spread and is sometimes categorized with them. It uses different strike prices and different expiration dates.
      • Horizontal Calendar Spread: Uses the same strike price, only difference being expiration date.

    • Example (NVDA): NVDA is trading at $300. You sell one NVDA July $300 call option and buy one NVDA August $300 call option. You’re betting that NVDA’s price will remain relatively close to $300. If NVDA stays near $300, the July call will lose value faster than the August call, and you’ll profit. If NVDA has a very big move, you will likely lose money.

    • Considerations:

      • Time decay: Near-term options decay faster.
      • Volatility changes: Longer-term options are more sensitive.
      • Underlying price movement: Large price swings can lead to losses.

  • Ratio Spreads: Ratio spreads involve buying and selling options of the same type (calls or puts) with the same expiration date, but with different strike prices, and in different quantities. The goal is to profit from the relative price movement of the options due to changes in the underlying’s price.

    • How it works: You buy or sell a greater quantity of one option than the other. This creates an imbalance in your position that can be profitable if your price prediction is accurate.

    • Types:

      • Bull Ratio Spread: Generally involves buying more calls at a lower strike price and selling fewer calls at a higher strike price. Profit if prices increase but is capped due to the short calls.
      • Bear Ratio Spread: Generally involves buying more puts at a higher strike price and selling fewer puts at a lower strike price. Profit if prices decrease but is capped due to short puts.
      • Backspread: Typically involves selling fewer options and buying more options. Backspreads are typically a play on a large move in the underlying security, and profits can theoretically be unlimited.

    • Example (AMD): AMD is trading at $100. You buy two AMD $95 calls and sell one AMD $105 call (2x1 ratio). If AMD rises, you profit due to increased profits of the two long calls versus the one short call. Maximum profit is capped due to the short call.

    • Considerations:

      • Directional bias: These spreads generally have a bullish or bearish bias.
      • Risk profile: The risk profile can be complex and depends on the specific ratio.

3.2. Credit and Debit Spreads

Credit and debit spreads are classified based on whether you receive a net credit (premium) or pay a net debit when entering the position.

  • Credit Spreads: You receive a net credit (premium) when entering the trade. Your maximum profit is the credit received, and your maximum loss is the difference between the strike prices, less the credit received. Credit spreads are used when you have a neutral or slightly directional (bullish for put credit spreads, bearish for call credit spreads) outlook and expect the price of the underlying asset to remain within a certain range. The risk is limited, but so is the profit potential.

    • Types:

      • Bull Put Credit Spread: You sell a put with a higher strike price and buy a put with a lower strike price. You want the underlying price to stay above the higher strike price.
      • Bear Call Credit Spread: You sell a call with a lower strike price and buy a call with a higher strike price. You want the underlying price to stay below the lower strike price.

    • Example (META): META is trading at $250. You sell one META $245 put and buy one META $240 put, receiving a net credit of $2. You will keep your profit of $2 if META stays above $245. Maximum loss is $3 if META price goes below $240.

  • Debit Spreads: You pay a net debit (premium) when entering the trade. Your maximum profit is the difference between the strike prices, less the debit paid, and your maximum loss is the debit paid. Debit spreads are used when you have a directional (bullish for call debit spreads, bearish for put debit spreads) outlook and want to limit your risk compared to simply buying options.

    • Types:

      • Bull Call Debit Spread: You buy a call with a lower strike price and sell a call with a higher strike price. You want the underlying price to rise.
      • Bear Put Debit Spread: You buy a put with a higher strike price and sell a put with a lower strike price. You want the underlying price to fall.

    • Example (GOOG): GOOG is trading at $120. You buy one GOOG $120 call and sell one GOOG $125 call, paying a net debit of $3. Maximum profit ($2) will be earned if GOOG exceeds $125. Maximum loss ($3) will be incurred if GOOG price stays below $120.

3.3. Strategies Based on Implied Volatility (IV)

Implied volatility (IV) is the market’s expectation of how much the price of the underlying asset is likely to fluctuate in the future. It’s “implied” because it’s derived from the prices of options. Advanced traders often use IV to identify potentially overvalued or undervalued options.

  • How it works: Options with higher IV are more expensive. Traders who believe IV is too high might sell options, expecting IV to decrease (and option prices to fall). Conversely, traders who believe IV is too low might buy options, anticipating an increase in IV (and option prices).

  • Strategies:

    • Selling Volatility (High IV): You might sell straddles, strangles, or other options strategies when IV is high, betting that IV will decrease. Your potential profit is the premium received, and risk is substantial if price moves significantly in either direction.
    • Buying Volatility (Low IV): If you anticipate a big price move but are unsure of the direction, you could buy straddles or strangles when IV is low, betting that it will rise as the price moves. This is sometimes called a “long volatility” strategy.
    • Volatility Trading with Calendar Spreads: You might use calendar spreads to bet on changes in the term structure of volatility (how IV changes across different expiration dates).

  • Example (NFLX): NFLX is trading at $350, and IV is unusually high due to an upcoming earnings announcement. You believe the post-earnings move won’t be as volatile as the market anticipates. You could sell a NFLX strangle (sell an out-of-the-money call and an out-of-the-money put with the same expiration date) to profit from the expected drop in IV after earnings.

  • Considerations:

    • Predicting volatility: IV is difficult to predict with certainty.
    • Time decay: Options lose value as time passes, even if volatility remains unchanged.
    • Risk management: Volatility strategies can be risky, especially when selling options, as losses can be substantial.

4. General Considerations for Advanced Strategies

  • Experience: These strategies are best suited for traders with significant experience in options trading.
  • Risk Management: Thorough understanding of risk is crucial. Implement risk mitigation strategies (like stop-loss orders or hedging).
  • Paper Trading: Practice using these strategies in a simulated environment before risking real capital.
  • Market Conditions: Always consider current market conditions, economic events, and news that might affect the underlying asset’s price and volatility.

These advanced strategies provide more sophisticated tools for options trading, but they also come with greater complexity and risk. Thorough research, constant learning, and careful risk management are paramount.

Important Note: This is just an introduction to the world of option strategies. Mastery requires continuous learning, practice, and a keen understanding of risk management. Always start with paper trading or small positions to get experience and refine your approach.

The examples provided are simplified illustrations. Real-world options trading involves complexities like commissions, bid-ask spreads, and the ever-changing nature of market conditions. Always conduct thorough research and consider your own risk tolerance before implementing any option strategy.
